Industry Deep Dive — DRAM/NAND Memory

1. Industry Definition

Semiconductor memory — DRAM (volatile, working memory) and NAND flash (non-volatile, storage), plus the fast-growing HBM (high-bandwidth memory) sub-category used in AI accelerators. GICS: Semiconductors & Semiconductor Equipment.

2. Market Size & Growth
  • Global memory market: ~$203B (2026E) → ~$403B by 2033 (CAGR ~10.3%), per Persistence Market Research.
  • WSTS: total semiconductor market ~$975B in 2026 (+26.3% YoY); memory segment ~$295B (+39.4% YoY).
  • BofA "supercycle" view: 2026 DRAM revenue +51% YoY, NAND +45% YoY; HBM market alone ~$54.6B in 2026 (+58%).
3. Industry Structure

Oligopoly. DRAM: Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron together hold >90% share. NAND adds Kioxia and Western Digital/SanDisk to the top tier. Barriers to entry: VERY HIGH — multi-billion-dollar fabs, multi-year qualification cycles, deep IP/process know-how.

4. Value Chain Position

Integrated Device Manufacturer (IDM) — Micron designs and fabricates in-house. Pricing power: currently STRONG (supply-constrained upcycle) but historically swings to WEAK in downcycles. Customer switching costs: MODERATE (qualification cycles create some stickiness, but commodity DRAM/NAND remain price-competitive).

5. Porter's Five Forces
  • Threat of new entrants: LOW — capex and technology barriers are prohibitive.
  • Bargaining power of suppliers (equipment — ASML, Lam Research, etc.): MEDIUM-HIGH — EUV tool scarcity, export-control friction.
  • Bargaining power of buyers (hyperscalers, OEMs): MEDIUM-HIGH — top-10 customers are >50% of revenue, multi-year LTAs cut both ways.
  • Threat of substitutes: LOW — no near-term substitute for DRAM/NAND/HBM in compute architectures.
  • Competitive rivalry: HIGH — three-way oligopoly battles for share even within a supply-constrained market.
Overall Industry AttractivenessATTRACTIVE (cyclically, currently very attractive)
6. Key Industry Trends
  • HBM4 transition doubling I/O count, commanding >30% pricing premium.
  • Structural shift of DRAM demand mix toward data-center/server vs. consumer/mobile.
  • Three-way race for advanced node leadership (1-gamma DRAM, 200+ layer NAND).
7. Regulatory Environment

US CHIPS Act subsidies (tailwind); evolving US export controls on semiconductor equipment/tools to China (headwind/uncertainty); China's Cyberspace Administration scrutiny of US memory chips in China (flagged risk factor in 10-Q). Regulatory risk: MODERATE-HIGH and a genuine two-sided factor — a moat in the US, a threat in China.

8. Company's Positioning

Rank #3 globally in DRAM (22.4% share), top-tier in HBM execution, sold-out 2026 HBM capacity. Competitive advantage vs industry: ABOVE AVERAGE — strong execution and margin recovery, but trailing the two Korean leaders in scale and (per recent quarters) profitability growth rate.

Overall Verdict — 5-Year Strategic View

"Micron's medium-term compounding story is strong but expensively priced. The AI-driven memory supercycle has structurally repriced earnings power — forward multiples (10-16× P/E, 0.37 PEG) look genuinely cheap relative to the semiconductor sector. But the stock has already moved 783%+ in 52 weeks, trailing multiples are stretched, and memory remains a brutally cyclical industry that has wiped out similar rallies before (FY2023 swung to a $5.8B loss). The key risk to monitor over a 5-year horizon is whether the current AI capex super-cycle proves structural or whether a familiar DRAM/NAND boom-bust repeats once new global capacity (Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron's own $25B FY26 capex) comes fully online around 2027-2028."
